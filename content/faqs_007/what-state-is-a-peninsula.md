A peninsula is a piece of land that extends out into a body of water, such as a lake, an ocean, or a river. It is surrounded by water on three sides and is connected to a larger landmass on its fourth side. Peninsulas are usually created by glaciers, rivers, or erosion, but they can also be man-made.

<h2>Geography of Peninsulas</h2>

Peninsulas come in many shapes and sizes, from the long, thin peninsulas of Scandinavia to the vast and heavily populated Iberian Peninsula. They can be found on every continent in the world and are often home to a variety of climates and ecosystems.

The geography of a peninsula is usually determined by its size, shape, and location. For example, peninsulas in the northern hemisphere tend to be warmer and have more temperate climates than those in the southern hemisphere. The size of a peninsula also affects its geography, as larger peninsulas may have higher mountains and larger bodies of water.

<h2>Types of Peninsulas</h2>

There are three main types of peninsulas: coastal, island, and riverine. Coastal peninsulas are those that extend into a body of water and are bordered by land on all sides. Island peninsulas are surrounded by water on all sides, while riverine peninsulas are bordered by rivers or streams.
