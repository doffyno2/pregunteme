Nonmaleficence is a core ethical principle in healthcare that requires practitioners to do no harm to their patients. It is closely related to the Hippocratic Oath and is a fundamental concept in medical ethics. The principle of nonmaleficence is based on the idea that healthcare providers should not intentionally harm or inflict suffering on patients, but instead should act in the best interests of their patients’ health and wellbeing.

<h2>Overview of Nonmaleficence</h2>

The term nonmaleficence is derived from the Latin phrase “non-male-feci” which means "do no harm". It is closely related to the Hippocratic Oath, a code of ethics in medicine, which states that physicians should “above all, do no harm”. Nonmaleficence is one of the four main ethical principles in healthcare, along with beneficence, autonomy, and justice.

Nonmaleficence is a fundamental principle in healthcare ethics because it requires healthcare providers to do no harm or inflict suffering on patients. It is important to note that nonmaleficence does not mean that healthcare providers should never cause harm or suffering, but rather that they should act in the best interests of their patients’ health and wellbeing.
