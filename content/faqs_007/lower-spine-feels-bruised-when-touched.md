<p>Lower back pain is a common complaint that affects millions of people. It can range from a dull ache to a sharp, stabbing sensation. Depending on the cause, lower back pain can be mild or severe, or even chronic. If you experience lower back pain that persists or worsens, it’s important to see your doctor.</p>

<h2>What Causes Lower Back Pain?</h2>

<p>Lower back pain can be caused by a variety of factors. Some common causes include:</p>

<ul>
    <li>Muscle strain</li>
    <li>Injury or trauma</li>
    <li>Overexertion</li>
    <li>Poor posture</li>
    <li>Degenerative conditions such as osteoarthritis or spinal stenosis</li>
    <li>Herniated or slipped discs</li>
    <li>Spinal fractures</li>
    <li>Infections</li>
    <li>Tumors</li>
</ul>

<h2>What Are the Symptoms of Lower Back Pain?</h2>

<p>The symptoms of lower back pain vary depending on the cause. Generally, however, lower back pain may include:</p>

<ul>
    <li>A dull ache</li>
    <li>Stabbing or sharp pain</li>
    <li>Pain that worsens with movement or pressure</li>
    <li>Pain that radiates down the legs</li>
    <li>Muscle stiffness or spasms</li>
    <li>Numbness or tingling in the legs</li>
    <li>Weakness in the legs</li>
</ul>

<h2>How Is Lower Back Pain Diagnosed?</h2>

<p>Your doctor will ask you questions about your lower back pain and may perform a physical exam. X-rays, CT scans, and MRIs may also be used to help diagnose the condition and determine the cause. Blood tests may also be performed to rule out any other medical conditions.</p>

<h2>How Is Lower Back Pain Treated?</h2>

<p>Treatment for lower back pain varies depending on the cause. However, some common treatments include:</p>

<ul>
    <li>Rest and activity modification</li>
    <li>Physical therapy</li>
    <li>Medication such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) or muscle relaxants</li>
    <li>Steroid injections</li>
    <li>Surgery for herniated discs or other conditions</li>
</ul>
