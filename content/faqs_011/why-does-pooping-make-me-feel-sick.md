<h2>What Causes Pooping to Make You Feel Sick?</h2>

<p>There are several potential causes for pooping to make you feel sick, some of which are more serious than others. Let’s take a look at some of the most common causes:</p>

<h3>Constipation</h3>

<p>Constipation is one of the most common causes of feeling sick after pooping. When you’re constipated, your stool is harder and drier than normal. This makes it harder to pass and can cause cramping or discomfort. Additionally, constipation can cause gas to build up in your intestines, which can cause bloating and nausea.</p>

<h3>Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)</h3>

<p>Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is a chronic gastrointestinal disorder that can cause abdominal pain, bloating, and changes in your bowel habits. IBS is often triggered by certain foods, stress, or hormonal changes. People with IBS may experience alternating bouts of constipation and diarrhea, and may feel sick after pooping.</p>

<h3>Gastroenteritis</h3>

<p>Gastroenteritis is an inflammation of the intestines that is usually caused by a virus or bacteria. Symptoms can include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea. People with gastroenteritis may feel sick after pooping due to the inflammation of the intestines.</p>

<h3>Infections</h3>

<p>Infections like bacterial gastroenteritis, urinary tract infections, and sexually transmitted infections can cause nausea and abdominal pain. People with these infections may feel sick after pooping due to the inflammation caused by the infection.</p>

<h3>Intestinal Blockages</h3>

<p>Intestinal blockages can be caused by a variety of factors like hernias, tumors, or scar tissue. They can cause abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. People with intestinal blockages may feel sick after pooping due to the obstruction in the intestines.</p>

<h2>What Can You Do to Alleviate the Discomfort?</h2>

<p>The best way to alleviate the discomfort of feeling sick after pooping is to identify and address the underlying cause. If the cause is constipation, it is important to increase your intake of fiber and water to help keep your stool soft and easy to pass. Additionally, taking a stool softener or laxative can help alleviate constipation. If the cause is IBS, it is important to identify and avoid any triggers that may be causing your symptoms. Additionally, medications like antispasmodics and antidepressants can help reduce symptoms. If the cause is an infection, it is important to seek medical treatment. Finally, if the cause is an intestinal blockage, it is important to seek medical attention as soon as possible.</p>
