Fragua food is a traditional cuisine from the Mexican state of Michoacan. It is a combination of ingredients and cooking methods that has been used for centuries by the people of the region. Fragua food is characterized by its use of chili peppers, fresh herbs, and unique spices, as well as its slow-cooking methods. The cuisine has been widely adopted in Mexico, as well as other parts of Latin America, and has become popular beyond the region.

<h2>History of Fragua Food</h2>

Fragua food has its roots in the pre-Columbian era, when the indigenous people of the region used to cook their food in the fragua (clay oven). This method of cooking is still used today, and the fragua is a symbol of the Michoacan region. The ingredients and cooking methods used in Fragua food are traditional to the region, and have been passed down through generations.

The flavors and textures of Fragua food are unique, and the cuisine has become increasingly popular in Mexico and beyond. The food is cooked slowly and carefully, allowing the flavors to develop and blend together. The use of chili peppers, herbs, and spices adds depth and complexity to the dishes.

<h2>Ingredients in Fragua Food</h2>

Fragua food is known for its use of traditional ingredients, many of which are native to the region. Common ingredients include corn, beans, squash, chilies, garlic, onions, tomatoes, and several herbs and spices. The cuisine also commonly uses ingredients such as pork, chicken, and beef, as well as seafood.

<h2>Cooking Methods of Fragua Food</h2>

Fragua food is cooked slowly over a low heat, allowing all the flavors to blend together. This cooking method creates a unique flavor profile, as the ingredients are given time to infuse together. Many dishes are cooked in the fragua, which is a clay oven, as well as in a traditional pot over an open fire.
