<h2>What You'll Need</h2>

<p>Before you start counting links on a bicycle chain, you'll need a few supplies. You'll need a chain tool, a rag, and some lubricant. A chain tool is a device used to remove and install bicycle chain links, and it's an essential tool for all cyclists. You'll also need a rag to clean off any dirt, grime, and oil from the chain before you start. Once the chain is clean, you can lubricate it with a lubricant of your choice.</p>

<h2>Step One: Place the Chain on a Flat Surface</h2>

<p>The first step is to place the chain on a flat surface. This will make it easier to count the links, as they won't be overlapping each other. Once the chain is laid out flat, you can begin counting.</p>

<h2>Step Two: Count the Inner Plate Links</h2>

<p>Now it's time to start counting the links on the chain. Begin by counting the inner plate links, which are the links that run along the inside of the chain. Count from one end of the chain to the other, making sure to count each link separately. When you reach the other end of the chain, record the total number of inner plate links.</p>

<h2>Step Three: Count the Outer Plate Links</h2>

<p>Once you've finished counting the inner plate links, it's time to count the outer plate links. These are the links that run along the outside of the chain. Begin at one end and count each link separately until you reach the other end. Again, record the total number of outer plate links.</p>

<h2>Step Four: Add the Links Together</h2>

<p>Once you've finished counting the inner and outer plate links, it's time to add them together. This will give you the total number of links on the chain. If the total number is less than the recommended number for the size of your chain, then it's time to replace the chain. If the total number is higher than the recommended number, then the chain is likely still in good condition.</p>
